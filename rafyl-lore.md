# The Chronicle of Rafyl the Highwarden

## I. The Grassy Hills of Rasenberg

In the northeastern reaches of the known world, far from the great cities of men and the glittering courts of high elves, there lies a land called **Rasenberg** — the Grassy Hills.

It is not a kingdom. It has no throne, no crown, no banner that flies above castle walls. Rasenberg is a stretch of endless green ridges and windswept valleys, where the grass grows tall enough to hide a man on horseback and the sky stretches so wide it presses down on the soul like a second gravity. Ancient stone markers — carved in Primordial script older than any living tongue — dot the hilltops like broken teeth. The wind never stops. It howls through the valleys at night, and the elves who live there say the wind carries voices, if you know how to listen.

The elves of Rasenberg are not like their kin.

They are not the graceful, willowy creatures of the great forests. They are not the aloof high elves of marble towers. The Rasenberg elves are **hill folk** — tall, broad-shouldered, weathered by wind and sun. They are closer to the land than to the stars. They farm the valleys, herd goats along the ridgelines, and build their homes half-buried into the hillsides, with turf roofs and stone hearths that burn peat through the long winters. They speak Elvish with a rolling, guttural accent that city elves find barbaric. They also speak Primordial — the ancient language of elemental forces — because the stone markers on the hills are written in it, and the elders insist that every child learn to read the old words before they learn to hold a blade.

Rasenberg has no army. It has **Wardens**.

The Wardens are an order as old as the hills themselves — part ranger, part scholar, part holy sentinel. They patrol the borders, maintain the ancient markers, and guard the knowledge stored in the deep libraries carved into the bedrock beneath the largest hill, **Aethelhyrst** — the Scholar's Rest. They answer to a council of elders, not a king. Their authority comes not from blood or conquest, but from oath and duty.

It is a quiet place. A forgotten place.

And that is exactly how the Wardens want it.

## II. The House of Thandril

Rafyl was born in a low-roofed stone house on the western slope of Aethelhyrst, the eldest child of **Thandril** and **Aelindra**.

**Thandril** was a Warden — one of twelve who served at any given time. A serious, silent elf who stood nearly six and a half feet tall and carried himself like a man perpetually bracing against a storm. He was not a warrior of great renown. He was a patrolman, a border-walker, a man who spent weeks at a time alone in the hills checking the ancient markers for signs of weathering or tampering. He spoke little, read much, and believed — with a conviction that bordered on obsession — that the old knowledge carved into the stones was not myth or poetry but **literal truth**. The stones, he told his children, were warnings left by beings who understood forces that modern minds had forgotten. He was respected by the other Wardens but considered eccentric by the wider community. "Thandril talks to stones," the village wives would say, not unkindly.

**Aelindra** was the opposite. She was a healer — practical, warm, sharp-tongued, and impatient with anything she considered nonsense. She came from a farming family in the southern valleys and had married Thandril against her parents' wishes. ("You're wedding a man who loves rocks more than people," her mother had said. She was not entirely wrong.) Aelindra ran the household, tended the sick, and kept the family fed while Thandril wandered the ridgelines. She was beloved in the community. Where Thandril was distant, Aelindra was present — always at someone's door with a poultice or a pot of stew, always ready with a cutting remark that somehow left people laughing instead of offended.

Rafyl had one sibling: a younger sister named **Sylwen**, born six years after him. Where Rafyl was large and serious even as a child, Sylwen was quick, small, and mischievous. She would grow up to be a goatherd in the southern valleys, content with a simple life — everything Rafyl was not.

## III. A Child Too Large and Too Loud

From birth, Rafyl was unusual.

He was big. Bigger than any elven child the midwives had seen. By the time he was ten — still practically an infant by elven reckoning — he was already the height of a grown human, with broad shoulders, thick hands, and a frame that seemed built for hauling stone rather than dancing through moonlit glades. The other elven children called him **Grashyrde** — "Grass-ox" — because he moved through the tall grass like a draft animal, trampling everything in his path. He was not graceful. He was not quick. He knocked things over. He tripped on roots. He could not thread a needle or string a bow to save his life.

His dexterity was, frankly, terrible. And in a culture that valued precision — in craftsmanship, in archery, in the elegant Elvish calligraphy used to annotate the ancient texts — this was a mark against him.

But Rafyl had two gifts that no one could deny.

The first was his **mind**. He learned to read before he could properly run. By fifteen, he had devoured every text in the village lending-library. By twenty, he was spending his nights in the deep archives beneath Aethelhyrst, reading scrolls that most Wardens didn't touch until their second century. He absorbed languages the way other children absorbed games. He could read Primordial fluently by twelve. He asked questions that made the elders uncomfortable — not because they were disrespectful, but because they were too sharp. "If the stones are warnings," he asked the elder Warden Calathis once, "then who are they warning? And why did the ones who carved them leave?"

Calathis had no answer.

The second gift was his **strength**. What Rafyl lacked in grace, he made up for in raw physical power. He could lift stones that took two grown elves to carry. He could drive a fence post into frozen ground with three strikes. When the older boys tried to bully him for his clumsiness, he didn't dodge or parry — he simply grabbed them. Once was usually enough.

His mother saw these gifts clearly. "You have your father's mind and a body the hills built for you," she told him. "Use both, or you'll waste both."

His father said less, but what he said stuck harder. One evening, when Rafyl was perhaps twenty-five, Thandril returned from a long patrol and found his son reading a Primordial text by lamplight. He stood in the doorway for a long moment, then said: "The truth doesn't care if you're graceful, boy. It only cares if you're strong enough to carry it."

Rafyl never forgot that.

## IV. The Sage's Path

At thirty — still a youth by elven measure, barely an adolescent — Rafyl was formally apprenticed to the archives beneath Aethelhyrst.

This was unusual. The archives were typically staffed by elves well into their second century. But the head archivist, an ancient elf named **Maethor**, saw something in Rafyl that he couldn't ignore. "The boy reads Primordial better than I do," he told the council. "And he asks the questions I stopped asking two hundred years ago because they frightened me."

The archives were Rafyl's true home. They were carved deep into the hill — a labyrinth of stone corridors lined with shelves of scrolls, bound books, clay tablets, and crystal-etched plates that hummed faintly when touched. The air smelled of old paper, lamp oil, and the mineral tang of deep earth. Light came from phosphorescent moss cultivated in stone troughs along the ceiling.

Here, Rafyl earned his **Sage's credentials**. He studied history, arcana, theology, natural philosophy, and the Primordial inscriptions that the Wardens had catalogued for millennia. He became proficient in the arcane arts — not as a practitioner, but as a scholar who understood magical theory the way an engineer understands physics. He could identify a spell by its residue. He could read a ward's construction and tell you who cast it and when. His knowledge of history was encyclopedic, and he had an almost unsettling ability to connect events separated by centuries into coherent patterns.

But the archives also sharpened his worst quality.

Rafyl was **blunt**. Painfully, socially catastrophically blunt.

It was not malice. It was the opposite — he genuinely wanted to help people understand things, and he could not fathom why they took offense when he corrected them. "I'm not insulting you," he would say, baffled, after reducing a senior Warden to sputtering rage by casually dismantling his theory about a Primordial inscription. "I'm just telling you you're wrong. There's a difference."

There was not, in most people's experience, a difference.

His mother tried to teach him diplomacy. It didn't take. "You have your father's tongue," she said, which was not a compliment. "You say true things in the worst possible way."

Aelindra herself was sharp-tongued, but she had charm to soften it — a warmth, a wink, a way of making the sting feel like affection. Rafyl had none of that. He stated facts like he was reading them from a stone tablet, and he could not understand why people found this hurtful. His charisma was, by elven standards, underwhelming. He was not unlikeable — people respected his knowledge and his willingness to help — but he was exhausting to talk to for more than a few minutes.

"Logic," he would say when challenged. "Emotions must not cloud our logical thinking."

He believed this absolutely. It was his north star, his organizing principle. And it made him very good at scholarship and very bad at friendship.

## V. The Monotheist

The Rasenberg elves practiced a faith that was unusual in the wider world.

Most elven cultures venerated a pantheon — Corellon, Sehanine, the Seldarine. The elves of Rasenberg acknowledged these names but did not worship them. They held, based on the oldest Primordial inscriptions, that behind all divine manifestations there was a **single creative force** — an intelligence so vast and so fundamental that naming it was an act of reduction. The elders called it **Aen** — a Primordial word that meant, roughly, "the source of what is."

This was not a popular theology. High elves considered it primitive. Humans found it confusingly abstract. Even among the Rasenberg elves, the depth of commitment varied — most treated it as cultural tradition rather than living faith.

Rafyl took it seriously.

Not because he was raised to. His mother was indifferent to theology. His father believed in the old knowledge but expressed his faith through action — through the maintenance of the stones, through duty — rather than through prayer or contemplation.

Rafyl came to his faith through the archives. The deeper he read, the more he found a coherent pattern beneath the Primordial texts — a unified framework that pointed toward a single originating will behind creation. It was, for him, a logical conclusion, not an emotional one. The evidence, as he saw it, was carved into the hills themselves.

"I don't have faith," he told Maethor once. "I have a conclusion based on available evidence."

Maethor, who had lived long enough to know better, just smiled.

But there was something else — something Rafyl would not have admitted, because admitting it would have meant acknowledging a feeling rather than a thought. In the deep hours of the night, alone in the archives, reading by lamplight while the phosphorescent moss hummed above him, Rafyl sometimes felt a **presence**. Not a voice. Not a vision. A weight. A warmth. The sense that something vast was watching — not with judgment, but with patience. As if the source of all things had noticed him and was waiting to see what he would do.

He never spoke of this. But it was real, and it changed him.

## VI. The Death of Thandril

When Rafyl was thirty-seven, his father did not return from a patrol.

Search parties found Thandril's body on the far western ridge, near a cluster of ancient markers that had been **defaced**. The Primordial inscriptions had been chiseled away — not by weather, but by tools. Thandril had been killed by a blade. Not an animal. Not a fall. A deliberate, precise wound through the chest.

There were no tracks. No witnesses. No explanation.

The council investigated for months and found nothing. The official conclusion was that Thandril had encountered bandits — human raiders from the lowlands who sometimes strayed into the hills. Case closed.

Rafyl did not accept this.

He examined the defaced markers himself and found that the inscriptions removed were not random. They were specific passages — **warnings** about something the Primordial texts called the *Ithren Vel*, the "Quiet Corruption." References to a force that worked not through armies or monsters but through **deception, influence, and the slow erosion of truth**. Someone had deliberately targeted these passages. Someone who knew Primordial. Someone who understood what the markers said and wanted that knowledge destroyed.

Bandits didn't read Primordial.

Rafyl brought his findings to the council. The elders listened. Then they told him, gently, that grief was clouding his judgment. That he was seeing patterns where there were none. That he should mourn his father and return to his studies.

"Logic," Rafyl said, his voice tight with something he refused to call anger. "Emotions must not cloud our logical thinking. I am not grieving. I am reading evidence. And the evidence says my father was murdered by someone who wanted specific knowledge destroyed."

The council did not change its conclusion.

Rafyl never forgave them for that.

And something shifted in him. The institutions he had trusted — the council, the elder Wardens, the careful hierarchies of Rasenberg — had failed. They had chosen comfort over truth. They had looked at evidence and decided it was easier to ignore it than to face what it meant.

If the structures meant to protect truth could not be trusted to pursue it, then what good were they?

This was the fracture that would define him. A man whose core ideal was logic and order, living in a world where the institutions of order had proven themselves cowards. It made him something contradictory — a believer in law who had lost faith in the lawmakers. A scholar of systems who trusted no system but his own judgment.

**Chaotic Neutral**, the philosophers might call it. Though Rafyl would have rejected the label. He would have said he was the only one being logical.

## VII. The Sealed Chamber

Rafyl threw himself into the archives with a fury that worried Maethor.

He was looking for something specific now. Every reference to the *Ithren Vel*. Every passage about the Quiet Corruption. He cross-referenced inscriptions from dozens of stone markers, reconstructing the defaced passages from fragments and parallel texts. He barely slept — though, being an elf, he didn't need much. Four hours of trance per night, and he was back at his desk, ink-stained and red-eyed and relentless.

What he pieced together disturbed him.

The Primordial texts described a pattern that repeated throughout history. Civilizations rose, flourished, and then rotted from within — not through invasion, but through a slow corruption of knowledge itself. Truths were altered. Histories were rewritten. The learned were discredited. The wise were silenced. And by the time anyone noticed, the damage was done. The texts called this the work of the *Ithren Vel* — not a single entity, but a **force**, a tendency, perhaps even a will, that fed on the destruction of truth.

And then Rafyl found a reference to something he had never seen mentioned before.

A sealed chamber, deeper than any part of the archives he had accessed. A room locked by the first Wardens, containing a text called **The First Chronicle** — described as the original and complete account of the *Ithren Vel*, written in Primordial by hands that had witnessed its work firsthand.

He asked Maethor about it.

The old archivist went pale. "That chamber is sealed for a reason, boy."

"My father was killed for a reason too."

Maethor said nothing for a long time. Then: "If you open that chamber, you cannot unread what is inside. And the knowledge will make you a target."

"I'm already looking for whoever killed my father. I'm already a target."

Maethor gave him the key. Not with approval. With resignation.

The chamber was small — barely larger than a closet. The air inside was stale and ancient, untouched for centuries. The Chronicle sat on a stone pedestal, bound in leather that had hardened to something like wood. The pages were thin metal sheets, etched in Primordial script so fine that Rafyl needed a magnifying lens to read it.

What he read confirmed everything he had suspected and added dimensions he had not imagined.

The *Ithren Vel* was not a metaphor. It was not a natural tendency. It was a **deliberate, ongoing campaign** — orchestrated by intelligences that operated in shadow, that wore the faces of trusted figures, that corrupted not through force but through the patient, systematic destruction of truth. The Chronicle described their methods in clinical detail. And it described the role of places like Rasenberg — isolated repositories of uncorrupted knowledge, the last redoubts against a war that most of the world didn't know was being fought.

The Chronicle also contained a warning: *The guardians of truth will be tested. The corruption will come for them. It has always come for them. And when it comes, the guardian must not hide behind walls. The guardian must carry the light into the dark.*

Rafyl closed the Chronicle. He sat in the sealed chamber for a long time, breathing stale air, thinking.

Then he picked up the book and carried it out.

## VIII. The Oath

What happened next is the part Rafyl does not speak about easily.

He will say that he prayed. This is unusual for him — he is not a man of ritual, and his faith is more intellectual than devotional. But alone on the hilltop above Aethelhyrst, holding the Chronicle, with the wind roaring around him and the stars blazing overhead, Rafyl knelt and spoke to **Aen** — the source, the creator, the intelligence behind all things.

He did not ask for power. He did not ask for vengeance.

He asked for purpose.

And something answered.

Not in words. Not in visions. In **certainty**. A weight settled into his bones. A warmth filled his chest. The wind died for a single, impossible moment — and in the silence, Rafyl understood what he was meant to do.

He would not be a scholar who studied truth from the safety of an archive.

He would be a **guardian** who carried truth into a world that was forgetting it.

He rose from his knees changed. The oath he swore was simple, spoken aloud to the wind and the stars and whatever listened behind them:

*I will protect the truth. I will carry it where it is needed. I will stand between the innocent and the lies that would consume them. I will not hide. I will not look away. And I will not stop.*

The power came immediately. Not much — a trickle, a warmth in his hands, the ability to mend wounds with a touch. **Lay on Hands**, the old paladins called it. A pool of healing light, replenished by rest and faith. It was modest. It was enough. It was proof that something had heard him and judged him worthy.

Rafyl the scholar became **Rafyl the Paladin**.

## IX. Preparation

He did not leave immediately.

He spent three years preparing. He trained with the Wardens in combat — not the elegant swordplay of the high elves, but the practical, brutal fighting style of the hill folk. He was never going to be quick. His reflexes were poor by any standard, let alone elven. But he was **strong**, and he was smart, and he learned to fight the way he studied — methodically, patiently, exploiting every advantage.

He chose a **longsword** — a weapon that rewarded strength and reach over speed. He drilled with it daily until his strikes were precise if not fast. He learned to fight in **chain mail** with a **shield**, accepting that he would never dodge what he could instead absorb. He practiced with **javelins** for range, since a bow in his hands was more danger to bystanders than to enemies.

He also prepared his kit with the methodical thoroughness of a scholar planning an expedition. **Ink and blank books** for recording what he found. A **holy symbol** of his faith — a simple disc of polished stone with the Primordial glyph for Aen etched into its surface. **Holy water**, because the texts suggested certain corruptions responded to it. A **lamp**, a **tinderbox**, a **blanket**, **rations**, **robes** for when armor would draw unwanted attention. A **small knife** for utility, not combat. Everything fit into a sturdy **backpack**, along with the Chronicle itself, wrapped in oiled cloth and warded with a simple prayer.

He said goodbye to his mother and his sister.

Aelindra did not cry. She held his face in her hands and said: "You are exactly like your father. And I mean that as both a compliment and a warning." Then she pressed a pouch of **gold** into his hand — thirty-eight coins, the family's savings — and told him to eat properly.

Sylwen punched him in the arm and told him not to get killed.

## X. The Departure

Rafyl stood before the council one final time.

He told them what he had read. He told them what he believed. He told them he was leaving Rasenberg to pursue the truth his father had died for.

The council was divided. Some called him reckless. Some called him brave. Elder Calathis — the same Warden who had failed to answer his childhood questions — simply nodded. "Your father would be proud," he said. "And terrified."

The council granted Rafyl the title of **Highwarden** — a rank not bestowed in living memory, reserved for Wardens who carried their duty beyond the borders of Rasenberg. It was part honor, part formality, and part the council's way of saying: *We cannot stop you, so we will at least make it official.*

At forty-two years old — barely an adult by elven reckoning, younger than any Warden who had ever held the title — Rafyl the Highwarden walked down the western slope of Aethelhyrst, through the tall grass, past the ancient stone markers, and into the wider world.

He carried a longsword on his hip, a shield on his back, six javelins in a quiver, a holy symbol around his neck, and the most dangerous book in Rasenberg wrapped in oiled cloth in his pack.

The wind followed him down the hill, and did not stop.

## XI. The Man Who Walks Between Worlds

Now Rafyl moves through a world he has only read about.

To those who meet him, he is an oddity. An elf who looks more like a human soldier — six foot six, two hundred and forty-five pounds, brown-haired and green-eyed, with light skin weathered by hill-wind. He wears a scholar's robe over chain mail. He speaks Common with a formal precision that sounds archaic, and Elvish with an accent that makes city elves wince. He will patiently explain anything to anyone, whether they want him to or not. He will tell you that you are wrong with the same tone he would use to tell you the time. He does not understand why this bothers people, and his attempts to understand only make it worse.

He is not charming. He is not diplomatic. He is not fast or graceful or subtle.

But he is **honest**. Relentlessly, sometimes painfully honest. And in a world where the *Ithren Vel* — the Quiet Corruption — works through lies and manipulation, honesty is its own kind of weapon.

He carries the Chronicle always. He reads it at night during his four-hour trance, turning the thin metal pages by lamplight, looking for connections to what he sees in the waking world. He records his own observations in ink, building a living companion text to the ancient one.

He heals the wounded with a touch. He fights when he must — without elegance, but with the steady, grinding determination of a man who has decided he will not fall. He asks questions that make people uncomfortable. He speaks truths that make people angry. And when he encounters lies — real lies, the kind that rot civilizations — he does not look away.

He is hunting something. He does not yet know its face.

But he knows it killed his father. He knows it is older than nations. And he knows that somewhere, in the margins of the world, it is still at work.

And the Highwarden will find it.
